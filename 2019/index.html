Modules and tentative schedule

  • Day 1: Operations on genomic intervals and genome arithmetic
  • Day 2: RNA-seq applications and analysis
  • Day 3: ChIP-seq applications and analysis

General Info

There will be theoretical lectures followed by practical sessions where students directly apply what they have learned. The programming will be mainly done in R.

The course will be beneficial for first year computational biology PhD students, and experimental biologists and medical scientists who want to begin data analysis or are seeking a better understanding of computational genomics and analysis of popular sequencing methods.

Students should expect to learn/get the following:

  • General structure and tasks in genomic data analysis
  • Performing operations on genomic intervals, finding nearest features, overlap operations, working with BAM files
  • Understanding pre-processing and quality check for sequencing data
  • Being able to perform differential expression and downstream analysis using RNA-seq data
  • Being able to perform peak calling and downstream analysis using ChIP-seq data
We have done similar courses in 2015, 2016, 2017 and 2018 . Here is a link to an article about the 2015 course.

Duration and Structure

The lectures and practicals will take the up full day. The candidates should plan for 9am to 6pm days. A typical day would comprise of lectures followed by hands-on exercises. There will also be additional scientific lectures showcasing application of computational genomics in current research projects.

A computer room with preset workstations will be provided. There will be one afternoon (mid-week) reserved for a social group activity

About the instructors

The instructors have decades of experience in data analysis for genomics. They are developers of Bioconductor packages such as methylKit, genomation and RCAS. In addition, they have been developing end-to-end genomics data analysis pipelines for RNA-seq, ChIP-seq, Bisulfite-seq, and scRNA-seq called PiGx
